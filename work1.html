Therapy Practice Management App

01/ Project Overview

As a part of the Google UX Design Certificate, the goal of this project was to create a holistic, data-driven, accessible, on-the-go, and fun-forward practice management app for mental health professionals.


There are so many aspects of a therapy practice that can be challenging to manage all at once, all the time. On top of client management, mental health professionals need to track items included in billing and scheduling to ensure their schedule and payments are in ship-shape.

Duration

June-July 2022

Role

Product Designer
product strategy, user research, wireframing, visual design, interaction, prototyping, usability testing

02/ Understanding the Problem

Foundational research consisted of conducting user interviews and competitive audits to test early concepts, better understand what problems actually exist in the space, and thereby create a useful value proposition.

The Problem

Therapists, mental health professionals, and clinicians struggle to manage clients, practices, billing, forms, and tasks, on top of their personal lives.

Industry Standards

Based on competitive audits, it became clear that many practice management applications lack everything needed to manage a practice, assistive tech for those with disabilities, and/or mobile apps as a service.

User Research

User interviews were conducted with a diverse sample of users to ultimately form empathy maps and affinity diagrams, which then allowed the team to deduce user pain points. Eclectic personas with overarching user stories and journey maps were created, cultivating a higher understanding of and empathize towards primary users of the app and clients affected by the app.


All of the above, in combination with four competitive audits, determine the optimal value propositions to differentiate our holistic therapy app.

Pain Points

  1. Time. Therapists are too busy to spend time with several overly complex apps.
  2. Accessibility. Platforms for client billing are not equipped with enough assistive technologies, particularly regarding the client portal.
  3. Information Architecture. Itâ€™s challenging for therapists to track insurance claims, therapy sessions, and billing all in one condensed location.
  4. Navigation between multiple apps. Disconnected, non-integrated apps and sites increase frustration and time away from patients.

03/ Starting the design

The design process followed an iterative and lean approach.

Key strategy & focus areas

The differentiating strategy was to position the product as the leading practice management app for therapists, clinicians, and other mental health professionals, by way of efficient and simplified:

  1. 1. Task oversight
  2. 2. Calendar organization
  3. 3. Billing administration
  4. 4. Client management (through contacts and messaging)

Wireframing

Paper wireframes were rapidly prototyped first through crazy eights and how might we methodologies, paired with additional brainstorming and storyboarding before becoming digitized.

Based on user goals and competitive audits, data-driven and holistic tools as well as on-the-go features and accessible tools were included in the digital wireframes, which were created to hone in on the visual structure.

04/ Refining the design

Post-wireframing, multiple usability studies were conducted to transform the design from wireframes to low-fidelity then high-fidelity mockups.

Usability studies

Usability studies were conducted before lo-fi mockups were drafted and after lo-fi mockups were drafted to create the final hi-fi design.


Through studies, it was discovered that additional accessibility considerations could be instilled in the app, billing and client contact pages are increasingly important for mental health professionals, and specific pages could be redesigned to allow for optimal navigation and connectivity.

Final Mockups

Hi-fi mockups were finalized once all data, themes, and insights were implemented.

Accessibility Considerations

The following considerations were integrated along the way and into the final designs: specific tools section which holds all features which connect to assistive-tech, specifically screen readers and additional text magnification; text considerations such as color modification and dark mode options for contrast changes and color blind friendly options; compatibility with various language options.
